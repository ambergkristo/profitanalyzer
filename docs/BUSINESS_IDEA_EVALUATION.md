# Business Idea Evaluation

## 1. Executive Verdict

**CONDITIONAL YES**

This is worth pursuing only if you treat it as a validation-first, consulting-led product and not as a clean SaaS build from day one.

The pain is real. Margin blindness is common. Owners do make pricing mistakes. But the category is not empty, the data problem is ugly, and "decision layer" by itself is not strong enough positioning unless you prove two things fast:

- small operators will actually maintain enough data to get value
- they will pay for action-oriented output instead of tolerating spreadsheets or broader back-office tools

If you try to build a polished app before proving those two points, you are likely to waste months on a product that feels smart but has weak buying urgency.

## 2. Problem Quality

### Is the pain real?

Yes. Restaurants often do not know true dish-level profitability with confidence.

### Is it urgent?

Sometimes. It becomes urgent when margins are squeezed, ingredient prices move, or a weak menu item sells heavily. Outside those moments, it drops behind staffing, service, and cash flow fires.

### Is it frequent?

Yes. Menu pricing, cost drift, and dish mix issues are recurring problems, not one-off events.

### Does the buyer already understand the pain?

Partially. Owners understand "food cost is bad" in a general sense. Many do not understand contribution margin at dish level, and many underestimate how much bad recipes or stale prices distort reality.

### Is this revenue-critical or nice-to-have?

Closer to revenue-critical than nice-to-have, but only when the output is tied to actions. Raw reporting alone becomes nice-to-have very fast.

Bottom line:

- the pain exists
- the math matters
- urgency varies
- buyer understanding is uneven
- action framing is mandatory

## 3. Customer Segment Evaluation

### Small and medium restaurants

Good segment for pain. Bad segment for data consistency. Many have imperfect recipes, ad hoc portions, supplier substitutions, and weak process discipline.

### Owner or manager buyer

Correct buyer. They feel margin pressure and can act. They are also overloaded and impatient.

### Willingness to pay

Possible, but not automatic. If the product feels like analytics software, willingness to pay drops. If it finds one clear pricing win, willingness to pay improves sharply.

### Data readiness

Weak. This is one of the biggest risks. Many restaurants will not have complete or current recipe data.

### Onboarding friction

High unless the first version allows manual, founder-assisted setup. Data cleanup is not a side issue. It is part of the product.

### Decision speed

Fast for owner-operated restaurants. Slow for groups, franchises, or businesses with multiple approvers.

### Sales difficulty

Moderate to high. The problem is understandable, but restaurant buyers are noisy, skeptical, and operationally distracted.

Bottom line:

- right buyer
- messy inputs
- possible willingness to pay
- high onboarding friction
- founder-led sales required first

## 4. Competition and Positioning

On April 25, 2026, a quick scan of official sites showed that current products already claim a lot of this territory:

- MarginEdge positions around food cost control, invoice pricing, menu analysis, and real-time price changes.
- meez positions around recipe management, live food costing, and menu engineering with scenario testing.
- Apicbase positions around menu engineering, supplier-linked costing, alerts, and sales analytics.

This matters because your proposed gap is narrower than it first appears.

### POS tools

Most POS tools are weak at deep costing, but they own transaction data and customer relationships. If they move further into margin analysis, your wedge gets thinner.

### Inventory tools

These already own purchasing and cost data. They may be clunky, but they have data gravity.

### Recipe costing tools

These are the closest competitors. They already offer costing, menu engineering, and in some cases scenario analysis and alerts.

### Spreadsheet workflows

Still the default competitor for small restaurants. Ugly, manual, but cheap and familiar.

### Why "decision layer" may not be enough

Because incumbents can also say "we help you make better decisions." That claim is easy to copy in marketing.

### Sharper niche positioning

A better wedge is:

- for independent restaurants, not chains
- for owner-managers, not finance teams
- setup done with you, not self-serve
- first answer in one session, not after full integration
- dish actions this week, not generic menu analytics

Sharper positioning line:

"Weekly margin actions for independent restaurants, without back-office software migration."

No external market-sizing statistics were used here. Competitor depth, pricing benchmarks by region, and market size still need separate research before any serious investment decision.

## 5. MVP Scope Assessment

### Dish cost calculator

Keep. This is non-negotiable.

### Margin calculation

Keep. Core output.

### Dashboard with KPIs and top actions

Keep, but simplify. Do not build a broad dashboard. Build a top-actions screen with a few KPI anchors.

### Dish table with margin status

Keep, but simplify. Focus on status, margin, sales volume, profit contribution, and action.

### Dish detail with ingredient breakdown

Keep. This is trust infrastructure.

### Simple what-if price simulator

Keep. This is one of the strongest demo features.

### Smallest commercial demo version

One dashboard, one dish list, one dish detail view, one price simulator, seeded with either manual or imported data for 10-20 dishes.

Anything beyond that before paid validation is unnecessary.

## 6. Commercial Validation Plan

### 14-Day Plan

#### Days 1-3

- build interview list of 30 local restaurants
- contact 20
- book 10 interviews

#### Days 4-7

- run 10 interviews
- conduct 3 manual mini-audits using real menus
- capture exact objections and pricing reactions

#### Days 8-10

- run 5 more interviews
- show a fake-door demo or clickable mock
- ask for paid pilot commitment

#### Days 11-14

- convert the strongest prospects into 2-5 pilots
- document why each yes or no happened

### Interview Count

- target: 15 qualified interviews in 14 days

### Exact Interview Questions

- How do you currently know which dishes are most profitable?
- When did you last change menu prices because costs changed?
- How do you notice when a popular item is low margin?
- Where do your recipe and ingredient costs live today?
- How often are those numbers updated?
- Who decides when a dish price changes?
- What is the hardest part of reviewing menu profitability today?
- If I showed you one item that is likely costing you money, what would you do next?
- Would you pay for a tool or service that gave you a weekly action list?
- Would you pay 49 EUR per month if it clearly found margin improvement?

### Manual Audit Workflow

1. Collect 2-3 dishes, prices, rough recipes, and recent ingredient costs.
2. Normalize units manually.
3. Calculate portion cost and margin.
4. Identify one pricing or recipe adjustment.
5. Estimate monthly impact using rough sales volume.
6. Deliver findings in a 10-minute review.

### Fake-Door Demo Idea

Landing page or lightweight mock with one promise:

"Upload 3 dishes. Get your first margin action in 10 minutes."

CTA options to test:

- Book free audit
- Reserve paid pilot
- Join early access

### Pricing Test

Ask three versions during calls:

- 29 EUR/month
- 59 EUR/month
- 149 EUR setup plus 49 EUR/month

Do not ask abstractly. Ask after showing real output.

### Success Criteria

- 15 interviews completed
- at least 5 restaurants agree to send real menu data
- at least 3 react strongly to findings
- at least 2 agree to a paid pilot or setup fee

### Failure Criteria

- owners say they already solve this well enough
- interviews reveal weak data and no appetite to maintain it
- no one agrees to share menu data
- no one will pay even after seeing concrete margin findings

## 7. Pricing Evaluation

### 29 EUR/month

Possible, but likely too cheap if onboarding is manual. Fine as a test anchor, dangerous as a long-term signal.

### 59 EUR/month

More credible for a single-location product if value is proven. Still probably low if setup effort is heavy.

### 99 EUR/month

Reasonable for multi-location lite, but not the first pricing question to optimize.

### Setup fee 100-300 EUR

This is not optional. It is probably necessary. Early customers are buying setup help and interpretation as much as software.

### Is pricing too cheap?

Yes, probably. Relative to the pain, 29-59 EUR can work. Relative to onboarding labor, it may be underpriced.

### Is pricing too expensive?

No, not if the product finds obvious margin upside. It becomes expensive only when value is abstract.

### What should be tested first?

Test `149 EUR setup + 49 EUR/month` first.

Why:

- aligns with manual onboarding reality
- filters unserious prospects
- still affordable for owner-operated restaurants

### Should the first version be SaaS, audit service, hybrid, or consulting-led SaaS?

Consulting-led SaaS.

Pure self-serve SaaS is the wrong opening move. The early value is in diagnosis, cleanup, and interpretation.

## 8. Product Risk

### Bad input data

- Severity: High
- Likelihood: High
- Mitigation: founder-assisted onboarding, stale-data flags, data completeness scoring, manual overrides

### Incomplete recipes

- Severity: High
- Likelihood: High
- Mitigation: allow partial-confidence outputs, highlight missing inputs, start with best-selling dishes only

### Ingredient price volatility

- Severity: Medium
- Likelihood: High
- Mitigation: last-updated dates, manual cost refresh flow, later supplier sync

### Owner unwillingness to change prices

- Severity: High
- Likelihood: Medium
- Mitigation: recommend recipe or mix actions, not only price increases; show profit impact clearly

### POS integration complexity

- Severity: Medium
- Likelihood: Medium
- Mitigation: defer integration, accept manual sales inputs first

### Low weekly usage

- Severity: High
- Likelihood: High
- Mitigation: position around weekly action review, alerts, and founder-led check-ins during pilot

### Product becoming "Excel with nicer UI"

- Severity: High
- Likelihood: High
- Mitigation: recommendation-first UX, tight dashboards, minimal tables, visible actions and impact above raw data

## 9. Technical Feasibility

### Stack

Feasible. React, Vite, Tailwind, Zustand, Express, Postgres, and Prisma are sensible for a solo founder MVP.

### DB schema

Feasible for MVP. Main concern is unit conversion and recipe completeness, not table count.

### Calculation engine

Straightforward. This is not technically hard. Correctness and traceability matter more than complexity.

### Decision engine

Very feasible as rules-based logic. Risk is weak output quality, not implementation difficulty.

### API scope

Reasonable for MVP. Keep it narrow.

### Feasibility for solo or founder MVP

Yes, if scope is cut aggressively and validation leads.

### Missing technical requirements

- unit conversion rules
- recipe yield handling
- stale data treatment
- partial data confidence model
- CSV import format
- sales volume date-range semantics
- manual override policy

## 10. UX and Product Experience Risk

### Useful in under 5 minutes

Possible only with guided data or founder-assisted demo. Not realistic for cold self-serve onboarding at first.

### Mobile usable

Possible, but discipline is required. Wide tables will ruin this quickly.

### Decision-first

Possible, but only if you resist dashboard bloat.

### Not Excel-like

Harder than it sounds. The domain naturally wants tables. You need to force action cards, ranked issues, and drill-down clarity.

### Premium enough to sell

Possible. Premium here means confidence and sharpness, not visual decoration.

UX is make-or-break because the math is not rare. The value comes from turning ugly margin analysis into an obvious operational next step. If the product feels like work, the buyer will go back to spreadsheets or ignore the tool entirely.

2. Dashboard with:
   - total dishes
   - number of warning or loss dishes
   - top 3 actions
3. Dish list with status and margin

5. Price simulator

- ingredient unit cost

- recipe yield
- dish price

### Exact Outputs

- margin percent
- margin amount
- estimated monthly profit contribution
- status label

### Demo Data Needed

- 10-20 dishes
- realistic ingredient costs
- rough sales volumes
- at least 3 clearly problematic dishes

### Manual Steps Allowed

### What Not To Build Yet

- supplier sync
- multi-location support
- automated alerts
- advanced menu matrix
- AI recommendation layer

## 12. Go / No-Go Gates

### Interview Gate

PASS:

- 15 qualified interviews completed
- at least 8 confirm the pain strongly

FAIL:

- fewer than 8 show real pain
- most say current tools are sufficient

### Demo Gate

PASS:

- at least 5 restaurants agree to review a real or fake-door demo
- at least 3 want their own data analyzed

FAIL:

- demos get curiosity but no follow-up or data sharing

### Pricing Gate

PASS:

- at least 2 prospects accept a paid setup or pilot structure

FAIL:

- everyone wants free analysis only

### Pilot Gate

PASS:

- at least 2 restaurants complete onboarding and use outputs in real decisions

FAIL:

- onboarding stalls because data is too messy or motivation is weak

### Retention Gate

PASS:

- after 30 days, at least 2 pilots still review the tool weekly or ask for continued audits

FAIL:

- usage collapses after initial curiosity
- no repeated decisions are driven by the product

## 13. Final Recommendation

### Decision

**Validate first**

Do not jump into building the full app. Sell the pain, run manual audits, and prove willingness to pay before you write significant product code.

### Top 5 Actions Next

1. Run 15 owner or manager interviews in Estonia.
2. Perform 3-5 manual mini-audits using real menus.
3. Test `setup fee + monthly` pricing after showing concrete findings.
4. Build a lightweight demo workflow, not a full platform.
5. Narrow the wedge to independent restaurants needing weekly margin actions.

### Top 5 Things To Avoid

1. Building integrations before proving demand.
2. Expanding into inventory or POS territory.
3. Shipping a dashboard full of charts with weak recommendations.
4. Assuming restaurants have clean recipe data.
5. Pricing so low that services work becomes unprofitable.

### Suggested First Codex Sprint After Evaluation

Documentation-backed validation sprint only:

- create outreach list
- draft interview script assets
- prepare a manual audit worksheet
- define demo dataset
- produce a clickable or spreadsheet-backed demo narrative

No application build should start before those validation tasks produce real buying evidence.
